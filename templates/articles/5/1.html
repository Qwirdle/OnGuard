Something many of us have seen, likely in movies, are the elusive Top Secret documents the protagonist is shocked at seeing. This is an example of Data Classification.

https://depositphotos.com

In the real world, people typically don't use Top Secret, instead opting to

  1. Confidential / Restricted / Level I

  2. Internal Use Only / Sensitive / Level II

  3. Public / Unrestricted / Level III

Confidential data is illegal to share publicly. This is called a data breach and is sometimes an example of breach of contract.

Data with a higher level of confidentiality should be treated with more care and caution as it likely contains sensitive data the public or a competitor should not know or cannot legally know. An example would be not transferring data over a public network. Confidential documents one is likely to see are:

  • Attorney-client communications

  • Medical records

  • Financial records

  • Patents

Additionally, data that is necessary or whose availability is needed should also be handled with care. Frequent backups are recommended.

As for how the data is handled, that varies between organizations and protection level. A good resource for finding what each protection level’s handling practices can be found here.

Information in classified documents may accidentally be leaked due to Social Engineering, making it important to let employees know what they can and can’t say. A lawyer is recommended if you want to find anything you can say and when.

Summary

  • Protected/sensitive documents must be handled with caution

  • It is illegal to share these documents to someone disallowed to view them.

  • If you or your company often deal with classified documents, get a lawyer.

  • Backup vital files
