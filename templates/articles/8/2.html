Organizational policies are a set of instructions or a specific way that something is done.

An organization will likely use these to make employees not mishandle data, what each role must be trained at, how to access systems, and how to respond to threats (these include Social Engineering, Denial of Service attacks meant to make servers go down, and many more).

These are important to mitigate risks, set expectations for employees, and to raise accountability in the workplace. They are also important for standardization, which is when something is made or done only a certain way. Standardization is important to not cause miscommunication and make sure data or files are not wrongly used or classified.

As previously stated, a common policy includes data classification, which make sure all data is classified the same way, mostly through sensitivity levels (Refer to 5.1 Understanding data classification and handling to see examples of different standards for data classification)

Companies may also require employees to change passwords and have strong passwords. Another important one is escalation, where one brings an issue or ticket to someone ranked higher or more specialized than them.

Summary

  • Different companies have different policies.

  • Most are meant to:

    • Heighten security

    • Heighten privacy

    • Handle issues/tickets more efficiently
